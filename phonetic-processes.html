Phonetic processes in connected speech

Hierarchical structure of syllables (σ). Key concepts:

  • Onset (first consonant or consonant cluster)

  • Nucleus (vowel)

  • Coda (last consonant or consonant cluster)

Phonemic transcriptions, also known as broad transcriptions and written between slashes //, are used to represent words using phonemes, the minimal speech units that convey sense and meaning in a language.

Phonetic transcriptions, also known as narrow transcriptions and written between square brackets [ ], can be used to represent more specific phenomena that tend to appear in connected speech, i.e. “real” language use, and which do not tend to result in changes in meaning. These representations are made via different symbols or diacritics proposed by the International phonetic alphabet. Here are some of the most common connected speech phonetic processes:

Linking / sandhi / liaison

In connected speech, whenever a word ends in a consonant and the following word starts with a vowel (i.e. C#V), the final consonant of the first word (i.e. its “coda”) tends to be articulated as the first consonant of the first syllable of the following vowel (i.e. its “onset”). This is rarely transcribed, especially in phonemic transcriptions //, but can be represented with the linking IPA symbol ‿ in phonetic transcriptions. For example:

I sat on the chair [aɪ ˈsæt‿ɒn ðə ˈtʃeə] ~ sounds like [ˈsæ tɒn]
I have a good friend [aɪ ˈhæv‿ə ˈgʊd ˈfrend] ~ sounds like [ˈhæ və]

This is particularly important in SSBE where /r/ is usually not pronounced in coda position, but is pronounced (i.e. it surfaces) in a C#V context:

💡 As a convention, linking-r is transcribed in both phonemic // and phonetic [ ] transcriptions.

my car / maɪ ˈkɑː / 🡪 my car is parked [maɪ ˈkɑːr‿ɪz pɑːkt]
here / hɪə / 🡪 here are my reports [ˈhɪər‿ɑː maɪ rɪˈpɔːts ]

A third type of linking sometimes occurs in a V#V context when the first V is a diphthong ending in /ɪ/ (/aɪ, eɪ, ɔɪ/) or /ʊ/ (/aʊ, əʊ/), or the vowels /iː/ or /uː/. In these cases, their closest corresponding approximant surfaces at the onset of the first syllable of the following word:

/ aɪ, eɪ, ɔɪ, iː / /j/ 🡪 [ʲ]
/ aʊ, əʊ, uː/ /w/ 🡪 [ʷ]

Who is it? 🡪 [ˈhuːʷ ˈɪz ɪt]
Now is the time! 🡪 [ˈnaʊʷ ˈɪz ðə ˈtaɪm]

Compression

Compression is a phonological process whereby two syllables, or sometimes two phonemes, become one. Two important cases of compression happen in spoken English:

Syllabic consonants (through schwa deletion)

nation, vision /ˈneɪʃn̩ ˈvɪʒn̩/
prism, chasm /ˈprizm̩ ˈkæzm̩/
simple, uncle /ˈsɪmpl̩ ˈʌŋkl̩/

In these cases, the sonorant, takes up the place of the syllable nucleus. This can be represented using the syllabic diacritic under the consonant [ ̩ ]. Furthermore, when the alveolar lateral approximant [l] becomes syllabic, it is also usually velarized (i.e. the back of the tongue approaches the velum). This “dark l”, which can also be found in coda position, can be represented with the symbol [ɫ].

simple, uncle [ˈsɪmpɫ̩ ˈʌŋkɫ̩]

Triphthong smoothing

In SSBE, the triphthongs / aʊə, aɪə, eɪə / tend to lose their median vocalic component and are “smoothed” towards the diphthongs [aə, eə]. Sometimes this can go even a step further and be pronounced as a long monophthong.

hour /ˈaʊə/ 🡪 [ˈaə] 🡪 [aː ~ ɑː]
fire /ˈfaɪə / 🡪 [ˈfaə] 🡪 [aː ~ ɑː]
player /ˈeɪə/ 🡪 [ˈpleə]

Aspiration

Voiceless plosives /p, t, k/ in syllable-initial position (=in onset position) are usually aspirated [ʰ] in stressed syllables:

pin /ˈpɪn/ 🡪 [ˈpʰɪn] apart /əˈpɑːt/ 🡪 [əˈpʰɑːt]
type /ˈtaɪp/ 🡪 [ˈtʰaɪp] attend /əˈtend/ 🡪 [əˈtʰend]
car /ˈkɑː/ 🡪 [ˈkʰɑː] occur /əˈkɜː/ 🡪 [əˈkʰɜː]

Aspiration does not happen when /p, t, k/ are preceded by /s/
spin /ˈspɪn/ 🡪 [ˈspɪn] a sport / ə ˈspɔːt/ 🡪 [ə ˈspɔːt]
scar /ˈskɑː/ 🡪 [ˈskɑː] a step / ə ˈstep/ 🡪 [ə ˈstep]

No audible release

At the end of words in C#C contexts or in C# contexts before pauses, plosives
/p, t, k, b, d, g/ tend to be articulated without their final release phase (the final “explosion” of the plosive which releases air). This is represented with the diacritic [ ̚ ] on top of the consonant.

I saw her at the concert 🡪 [aɪ ˈsɔː hər ət̚ ðə ˈkɒnsət̚ ]
“I’m tired,” he said. 🡪 [aɪm ˈtaɪəd̚ hi ˈsed̚ ]
bag 🡪 [ˈbæg̚ ] speak 🡪 [ˈspiːk̚ ] etc.

Nasalization

Vowels tend to become nasalized [ ̃] before or between nasal consonants /m, n, ŋ/ (i.e. in a VN or NVN context).

man 🡪 [ˈmæ̃n] morning 🡪 [mɔ̃ːnɪ̃ŋ] monkey 🡪[ mʌ̃ŋki]

Assimilation

Assimilation is a phonetic process whereby the distinctive features of one sound (place of articulation, manner of articulation, voicing) are transferred to a contiguous sound. Nasalization is one particular case of assimilation. Two other main cases are traditionally identified in English. These are cases of regressive assimilation, i.e. a sound A shares its features of a sound B in the following manner: B 🡨 A.

Assimilation of place

Alveolar consonants / t, d, n, s, z / are particularly affected by the place of articulation of the consonant that follows. Thus:

[ t, d, n ]:

[t] becomes [p] (bilabialized) before [p, b, m] (bilabial consonants), and
[k] (velarized) before [k, g ] (velar consonants)

💡 Some phonologists prefer using the “no audible release” diacritic on the assimilated phoneme because, when assimilated, these plosives tend to remain unreleased.

🡪 That car [ˈðæk̚ ˈkɑː]

Assimilation of manner

Rare in English.

Assimilation of voicing

Final voiced consonants tend to get devoiced [ ̥ ] or voiceless before an initial voiceless consonant:

🡪 a big person [ə ˈbɪg̥ ˈpɜːsən] or [ə ˈbɪk ˈpɜːsən]
have to [ˈhæv̥ tə] or [ˈhæf tə]
of course [əv̥ ˈkɔːs] or [əf ˈkɔːs]

Elision

In spontaneous connected speech, some clusters of obstruent consonants within or across words tend to get simplified for articulation’s sake. In clusters of 3 or 4 consonants, median consonants are prone to deletion:

I asked him 🡪 / aɪ ˈɑːskt ɪm/ 🡪 [aɪ ˈɑːst ɪm]
Next class 🡪 ˈnekst ˈklɑːs 🡪 [ˈneks ˈklɑːs]

Some words have integrated elisions into their standard phonological pronunciation:

must /ˈmʌst/🡪 mustn’t /ˈmʌsnt/
listen /ˈlɪsn/
grandmother /ˈɡrænˌmʌðə/

💡 This is how these words are represented in both phonemic and phonetic transcriptions.

Epenthesis

As opposed to elision, epenthesis is a phonetic process whereby a sound is inserted. In English, there is often an epenthetic post-nasal plosive (=an extra plosive after a nasal sound), especially in transitions from nasal sounds to plosives or fricatives. The inserted plosive is homorganic with the nasal sound (=they share the same place of articulation).

The most common cases are from /n/ to /s/ (alveolar to alveolar [nts]), from /m/ to /t/ (bilabial to alveolar [mpt]), from /m/ to /s/ (bilabial to alveolar [mps]). This happens because a small explosion tends to happen naturally during the transition between these sounds:

🡪 chance /ˈtʃɑːns/ 🡪 [ˈtʃɑːnts] or [ˈtʃænts] in GA
🡪 I’m stuck! /aɪm ˈstʌk/ 🡪 [aɪmpˈstʌk]
🡪 historically, empty is the result of epenthesis (added [p]): comes from Old English œmtig. (Laure Gardelle, 2013)
